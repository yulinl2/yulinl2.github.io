Current agent memory is text summaries: lossy, primitive, re-derived every turn. The bet is that weights, not text, are the better substrate — turning overparametrized rote memorization from a privacy bug into the mechanism. This dashboard collects what the literature actually says, and what we'd test.

The thesis, in 3 sentences

Fast to memorize, slow to understand

Surface memorization is fast — tens of steps, sometimes five. The conceptual internalization we actually want is slow, lagging by orders of magnitude and only appearing under the right regularizer. The number is set less by "the content" than by how much of the optimization we point at it.

Why now

The pieces just landed

Open 8B weights + LoRA make a trainable memory cheap. MemGen (latent memory, up to +38% over text memory) and GradMem (≤5-step context writes) are existence proofs from the last few months. The mechanism works — nobody has framed it as agent memory yet.

The honest catch

Two regimes, opposite sides

Faithful retention of specific content and internalization of a concept sit on opposite sides of the capacity threshold. We can't maximize both in one adapter at one scale — so the two scenarios are two differently-sized experiments, decided up front.

01

"Memorized" is three different events

No step-count means anything until the word is pinned down. The literature measures at least three things under one label — and they differ by orders of magnitude in how many steps they take to appear.

SENSE 01 · slowest

Verbatim regurgitation

The model emits an exact training sequence from a prefix. The strict privacy notion — "extractable with k tokens of context."

most demanding
SENSE 02 · the target

Fact / concept recall

The model answers a question or completes an association without reproducing wording. This is what 2-bits-per-param measures, and what a memory system actually wants.

our goal
SENSE 03 · fastest

Influence on behavior

A single example shifts outputs on related inputs, with no recognizable "storage." Captured by influence/counterfactual work. Can happen in one step.

one step

Why this is load-bearing, not pedantic: our economy condition (b) — "the small model fully retains the key information" — is implicitly asking for Sense 02. But the cheapest experiments default to measuring Sense 01 or Sense 03, because training loss and extraction probes reflect those most directly. A collapsed training loss demonstrates Sense-01 fitting and tells you almost nothing about Sense 02. The ROME work even locates these in different places: generalized "knowing" at the early-middle MLP site, rote "saying" at the late attention site. arXiv:2202.05262

02

The step-count, and the knobs that move it

Tirumala et al. defined the exact quantity — T_update(N, τ), the number of updates to reach memorization fraction τ. The headline cuts against using a small model: larger models memorize faster, monotonically. arXiv:2205.10770

VariableEffect on steps-to-memorizeWhy it matters here
Model size (N)↓ fewer  larger = faster, stronglyA 1B will be slower than an 8B; your small-scale step-count is an upper bound
Duplication / frequency↓↓ far fewer  log-linearYour single biggest lever, and it's free — duplicate the memory corpus deliberately
Learning rate↓ fewerTrades against stability of any generalization you want
Content type↓ fewer  for structuredNouns & numbers memorize first; facts write faster than prose
Threshold τ↑ many more  near completenessNo single number — a step-count per target fidelity; the curve steepens toward 100%

One genuinely encouraging finding for the premise — Tirumala's forgetting baseline: a model retains at least ~40% of data seen only once, even after training on other data for many epochs. Single-exposure content is not ephemeral. The three forces governing verbatim memorization, from Carlini, are clean: model size, duplication, and context length all increase extractability, each log-linearly. arXiv:2202.07646

03

Two regimes: five steps vs a thousand exposures

"≤5 steps" and "~1000 exposures" are both true, and the gap between them is the design insight: the step-count is a property of how much optimization you point at the content, not of the content itself.

1 stepsingle-step influence
≤5 stepsGradMem context write
~10²≈1 bit/param
~10³ exp.≈2 bits/param (max density)
~10⁶ stepsgrokking / generalization

Log-ish scale, illustrative. Left = concentrate all optimization on a small dedicated target (your architecture). Right = max density across a whole corpus during pretraining, or the delayed generalization transition.

The single-step regime is where your design lives. You're not pretraining on a corpus — you're writing a context into weights. GradMem does exactly this: arXiv:2603.13875 shows ≤5 gradient steps reliably write a context into a frozen model's memory params, and — decisively — a single gradient write stores more than a single forward pass, with forward-only repetition giving much weaker gains. That is concrete evidence the gradient route beats the in-context status quo. This sits inside the test-time-training / fast-weights family (Titans, TTT layers): a few gradient steps on a small dedicated target against a reconstruction objective.

Capacity sets the ceiling. Transformers store ~2 bits/param arXiv:2404.05405, or ~3.6 bits/param by a cleaner estimate arXiv:2505.24832; both find storage scales linearly with parameters. The crucial Morris finding is the bug-to-feature transition made mechanical: a model memorizes until capacity fills, at which point grokking begins and the model substitutes generalization for rote storage. Generalization is what memorization turns into when it runs out of room.

⚠ The structural tension to decide up front

Your Case 1 (retain noisy content faithfully) wants the model below capacity, where individual memorization works. Your Case 2 (internalize a concept) wants it pushed past capacity, where individual memorization fails and structure is forced. You cannot maximize both in the same adapter at the same scale — so they are two differently-sized experiments, not one.

04

The slow event: memorize fast, generalize much later

This is the single most important body of work for calibrating expectations, because it describes the gap between the fast fitting you'll see and the conceptual internalization you actually want.

Power et al. on modular arithmetic: training accuracy is near-perfect in under 10³ steps, but validation accuracy takes close to 10⁶ steps, with little generalization visible before ~10⁵. arXiv:2201.02177 Read the exponents — three orders of magnitude separate fitting the data from learning the rule that generated it. A model can sit at 100% train accuracy and 0% generalization for hundreds of thousands of steps before the rule clicks.

Mechanism

Norm minimization

The model memorizes by growing weight norm, then weight decay grinds norm down along the zero-loss manifold until the lowest-norm fit is the generalizing circuit. arXiv:2603.13331

Hard requirement

AdamW, not SGD

Grokking needs an optimizer that decouples memorization from contraction — SGD fails entirely at the same hyperparameters where AdamW reliably groks. Use an Adam-family optimizer with weight decay.

Facts version

3-phase plateau

Models learn facts in three phases with a plateau before acquisition; the plateau is when attention circuits form. Integrating new knowledge corrupts existing memories. arXiv:2503.21676

That last clause — new knowledge corrupting old — is an independent confirmation, from a different lab and setup, of the interference problem flagged elsewhere via Pletenev. It being robust across papers is exactly why we re-check retention on every write, not occasionally.

05

Where memory lives — and why read-out is hard

The dynamics connect back to condition (c), getting information cleanly back out, and the answer is constraining in a way that validates your own intuition.

The dominant picture: MLP layers act as key–value memories — a key encodes a subject, a value encodes knowledge, and a fact is localizable to MLP params at middle layers during the subject's last token. ROME / arXiv:2202.05262 This sounds like it should make read-out easy. Two findings break that hope, and they are the technical core of why condition (c) is hard:

a
Localization does not predict editability
disconnect▶
The layers a fact is causally localized to are statistically uncorrelated with where you must edit to change it. The place information "is" and the place you can manipulate it from are not the same — so knowing where a memory lives does not hand you a clean way to read it back out as text.
b
Superposition — no readable table
distributed▶
Facts are stored as overlapping linear combinations, not one-per-neuron. Recent mechanistic work: subject embeddings encode superpositions of attribute vectors, decoded on demand by a relation-conditioned selector. arXiv:2605.12426 You cannot enumerate what's stored by reading rows off a matrix — there are no rows, only directions in a vector space.
→ Consequence for the project

Your intuition — that concepts live inside the model and likely can't be explicitly reconstructed — is correct, and now you know why: the information is real and causally effective but stored in distributed, superposed, selector-decoded form. This is the precise reason to relax condition (c) from text-readable to latent-vector read-out: latent read-out (gist tokens, MemGen, GradMem) works with this format; text read-out fights it.

The two scenarios & what's actually feasible

Case numbering confirmed from the source thread: Case 1 = noise, Case 2 = concept. And the "big problem" — merge-back — likely dissolves rather than needs solving.

01

Noise filter

Lots of irrelevant noise, inconvenient to separate by hand.

  • Maps to LongMemEval — signal buried in ~115k tokens
  • Wants the model below capacity
  • Test: tiny adapter vs text summary vs raw long-context
02

Concept internalization

All content matters, but the useful thing is an internalized concept, not raw input.

  • Maps onto grokking — does it get the rule?
  • Wants the model pushed past capacity
  • Test: 50–200 raw examples → held-out instances, with weight decay

The merge-back question may be the wrong question. The one system that works end-to-end, MemGen, never merges back — it keeps a frozen base plus a detachable adapter routing latent memory. 07

Scale validity & how to run it

Which findings survive the trip from a small model to a real one, and the instrumentation that keeps the experiment honest.

Transfers small → large

  • Bits-per-param capacity — smooth from <1M to billions
  • Duplication / context laws — log-linear, stable
  • Larger = faster memorization ordering
  • Grokking phenomenology — tiny MLP to B-scale

Transfers poorly

  • Quality of internalized concepts — there's a floor below which the concept never forms
  • Exact quantities — "5 steps", "500 facts" are config-specific

Honest line: capacity, retention, forgetting, and the bare existence of a memorize→generalize transition are high-validity even at 1B. Anything concluding "this internalizes a frontier-useful concept" is low-validity below ~8B — treat a small win as "the machinery runs", never as "the hypothesis is true."

Stage plan — each with a hard go/no-go

Use an Adam-family optimizer with weight decay, duplicate the target corpus, dedicate params per-concept, and probe old content after every write. The weight-norm trace lets you kill a doomed run early instead of burning the compute.
